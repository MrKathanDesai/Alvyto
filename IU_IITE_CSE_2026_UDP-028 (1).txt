ABSTRACT


Alvyto is a fully local, AI-powered clinical automation system that transforms live
doctor–patient consultations into structured electronic medical records. The system captures
real-time audio from exam rooms, performs speech-to-text transcription using WhisperX (an
advanced extension of OpenAI's Whisper), applies speaker diarization via pyannote.audio to
distinguish doctor and patient speech, and generates structured clinical summaries using
phi4-mini, a compact instruction-tuned language model selected after a formal
benchmarking evaluation.
The summarization pipeline operates under a strict zero-inference rule: the language model
may only use words explicitly spoken during the consultation. Every generated fact is
subsequently validated against the source transcript using fuzzy string matching, with
unverifiable items flagged for doctor review. This design ensures that no AI-generated
content enters the patient's permanent medical record without human approval.
The system architecture comprises three services : a Next.js frontend, a FastAPI backend
managing EMR data and authentication, and a room agent handling the AI pipeline. The
backend implements PHI minimization by design: raw transcripts are never written to the
database and are erased after the doctor approves the summary. An immutable audit log
records all actions.
Model selection involved a structured benchmark across four candidates qwen2.5:3b,
qwen3:4b, phi4-mini, and llama3.1:8b. phi4-mini was selected for achieving a perfect
hallucination support rate of 1.00 and 100% schema compliance across all test cases, at a
warm inference speed of approximately 7.6 seconds on GPU and 30 seconds on CPU-only
clinic hardware.
Alvyto addresses the fragmentation between existing transcription tools, EMR systems, and
clinic management software by combining real-time audio processing, AI-driven
summarization, patient record management, and administrative workflows into a single
unified platform.

1.1 PROJECT SUMMARY


Alvyto is an AI-powered real-time clinical automation system designed to transform
doctor–patient interactions into structured electronic medical records. The system integrates
real-time audio capture, speaker diarization, speech-to-text transcription, and AI-based
summarization into a unified platform, enabling automated and efficient clinical
documentation.


During a consultation, the system continuously captures audio and processes it using
WhisperX, a state-of-the-art speech recognition framework that provides word-level
alignment and speaker-aware transcription. Speaker diarization via pyannote.audio
distinguishes between doctor and patient speech, ensuring clarity and proper structuring of
the conversation.


Beyond transcription, Alvyto incorporates an intelligent summarization module powered by
phi4-mini, a compact language model selected after formal benchmarking. The model
extracts key medical insights — symptoms, diagnoses, medications, and treatment plans and
generates a structured JSON summary. All generated facts are validated against the source
transcript using fuzzy string matching before being presented to the doctor for review.
End-to-end pipeline latency is approximately 70–100 seconds on CPU-only clinic hardware.


The system also includes patient record management, allowing doctors to access historical
data, review, edit, and approve AI-generated summaries before they are committed to the
permanent record. An integrated admin module supports clinic operations such as
appointment scheduling and room management.

1.2 PROJECT PURPOSE

The primary purpose of Alvyto is to automate clinical documentation by leveraging artificial
intelligence to process doctor–patient conversations in real time. In traditional healthcare
environments, doctors are required to document patient interactions manually, a process that
is time-consuming and reduces time available for patient care. Even with digital EMR
systems, data entry remains largely manual and repetitive.


Alvyto addresses this by
   ●​ capturing live conversations,
   ●​ converting them to structured text, and
   ●​ generating meaningful summaries automatically.


A key design principle is the zero-inference rule: the AI model is constrained to extract only
information explicitly spoken during the consultation, with every generated claim validated
against the transcript before being surfaced. This ensures accuracy and eliminates the risk of
AI hallucinations reaching the patient record.


The system also aims to improve consistency and quality of medical records by ensuring
structured, standardized output. By integrating administrative functionalities within the same
platform, Alvyto reduces the fragmentation that plagues existing healthcare tooling.

1.5 TECHNOLOGY AND LITERATURE OVERVIEW


   ●​ WhisperX: WhisperX (Bain et al., 2022) is an advanced extension of OpenAI's
      Whisper model that adds word-level timestamp alignment and integrates speaker
      diarization into the transcription pipeline. While standard Whisper produces
      segment-level transcripts, WhisperX aligns output at the word level using forced
      alignment, enabling precise attribution of each word to a speaker turn. The small.en
      model variant is used, operating with INT8 quantization for CPU-efficiency.
      WhisperX is suited to clinical environments because its diarization-aware output
      eliminates the need for a separate post-processing alignment step, reducing latency
      and improving speaker attribution accuracy in two-speaker doctor–patient scenarios.
   ●​ pyannote.audio: pyannote.audio (Bredin et al., 2023) provides the speaker
      diarization pipeline. The system uses silhouette scoring to adaptively determine the
      number of speakers without hardcoding an assumption — important in scenarios
      where a family member or nurse may be present in addition to the doctor and patient.
      Speaker segments are merged with WhisperX word-level timestamps to produce a
      structured, speaker-labelled transcript.
   ●​ phi4-mini: The summarization module uses phi4-mini, a 3.8B parameter
      instruction-tuned model from Microsoft Research. phi4-mini was selected after a
      formal four-model benchmark evaluation (see Section 7.1). The model receives the
      speaker-labelled transcript and returns a structured JSON object with four fields:
      clinicalSnapshot (4–6 verbatim clinical chips labelled by category), doctorActions
      (3–5 action strings), issuesParagraph, and actionsParagraph. The zero-inference
      constraint is enforced via a fuzzy matching algorithm that tokenizes each generated
      claim and checks whether at least 60% of tokens with length greater than 3 can be
      matched to the source transcript with a SequenceMatcher ratio above 0.72. Items
      failing this check are flagged as isSupported: false and visually highlighted in the
      doctor review UI. phi4-mini is served via Ollama, which provides cross-platform
      model    management      (Windows,         Linux,   macOS)   and   replaces   the   prior
      llama-cpp-python with Metal-specific configuration. Warm inference time is
      approximately 7.6 seconds on MPS and 30 seconds on CPU-only clinic hardware.

   ●​ FastAPI: The backend is built with FastAPI, a high-performance asynchronous
       Python web framework. FastAPI handles JWT authentication (HS256, bcrypt-12),

LITERATURE REVIEW
Speech recognition and AI-based documentation have evolved from rule-based,
limited-vocabulary systems to large-scale transformer models. Early systems required
structured grammars and failed on spontaneous conversational speech. The introduction of
CTC-based models and later attention-based transformers, culminating in Whisper's weakly
supervised pre-training on 680,000 hours of audio, achieved robust cross-accent and
noisy-environment performance.


AI in healthcare documentation has progressed from basic dictation tools (which require
structured input from the physician) to ambient intelligence systems (which operate passively
during the consultation). However, most existing commercial solutions are cloud-dependent,
raising significant PHI and data privacy concerns. Alvyto is fully local, no audio, transcript,
or patient data ever leaves the clinic network.


The literature consistently identifies the following unmet needs that Alvyto directly
addresses: real-time speaker-aware transcription during live consultations, structured
extraction of medical entities without relying on general-purpose NLP pipelines,
hallucination validation specific to clinical documentation, and integration of transcription
with administrative workflows in a single platform.

1.6 SYNOPSIS


Alvyto is a real-time AI-powered clinical automation system designed to eliminate manual
documentation during doctor–patient consultations. Audio is captured live from the exam
room and streamed via WebSocket to a locally running room agent. WhisperX transcribes the
audio with word-level precision while pyannote.audio attributes each segment to the correct
speaker. After the consultation ends, phi4-mini processes the diarized transcript and
generates a structured clinical summary in approximately 70–100 seconds on standard clinic
hardware.


The doctor reviews the summary on the exam room PC immediately after the visit. Any
AI-generated facts that cannot be verified against the transcript are highlighted for correction.
The doctor edits, approves, and the summary is committed to the permanent patient record.
The raw transcript is then erased — it never touches the database. An audit log records every
action without storing clinical content.


The system is fully local, requires no internet connection, and is designed for deployment on
a clinic-grade server with multiple room PCs acting as thin clients. The architecture is
modular: the frontend, backend, and room agent are independent services that can be updated
independently.

2.1 INTRODUCTION OF SURVEY


The literature survey examines existing technologies and systems in the domains of
automatic    speech recognition,     speaker diarization, clinical    NLP, and healthcare
documentation automation. The goal is to identify the state of the art, understand why current
solutions fail to meet the needs of active clinical environments, and justify the technology
choices made in Alvyto.


Speech recognition has advanced substantially since the introduction of deep neural network
acoustic models. Transformer-based architectures, particularly those trained on large
weakly-supervised corpora, have demonstrated robustness across accents, noise conditions,
and domains that earlier HMM/GMM systems could not handle. Whisper (Radford et al.,
2022) represents a significant milestone: a single model trained on 680,000 hours of
multilingual audio achieves near-human accuracy on multiple benchmarks without
domain-specific fine-tuning.


Speaker     diarization   has   similarly   matured.   Modern    neural    approaches   using
embedding-based clustering (x-vectors, ECAPA-TDNN) combined with overlap detection
achieve diarization error rates below 5% on two-speaker conversations in clean conditions.
pyannote.audio provides a well-maintained, state-of-the-art pipeline accessible through a
Python API, making it practical for integration into production systems.


AI-based clinical documentation has attracted significant research attention. Systems such as
Amazon Transcribe Medical and Nuance DAX offer cloud-based ambient clinical
intelligence, but their dependence on external APIs raises privacy and regulatory concerns,
particularly for smaller clinics that may not have the infrastructure for cloud EMR
integration. Fully local solutions remain rare.

2.2 WHY SURVEY?


The survey was conducted to answer three specific questions:
   ●​ which transcription technology best handles clinical conversational speech without
       cloud dependency;
   ●​ how to reliably validate AI-generated medical claims against a source transcript to
       prevent hallucinations from entering permanent records; and
   ●​ whether a compact LLM in the 3–4B parameter range can produce structured clinical
       output reliably enough for production use.


The analysis of WhisperX against standard Whisper confirmed that word-level alignment is
necessary for accurate speaker attribution, segment-level timestamps from standard Whisper
create misattribution errors at speaker transitions. The analysis of LLM candidates revealed a
critical failure mode in Qwen3:4b: its hybrid thinking architecture consumed the entire token
budget on internal chain-of-thought traces before producing output, rendering it incompatible
with token-budgeted structured output tasks. This finding, and the subsequent selection of
phi4-mini, is documented in the benchmark results in Chapter 7.


The survey also confirmed that no existing open-source system combines real-time
speaker-aware transcription, hallucination-validated summarization, and clinic workflow
management in a single fully local platform. This gap directly motivates Alvyto's design.

5.4 PROCESS MODEL


The Generic Data Reconciliation Application follows an iterative and modular process
model. This model was chosen because the application consists of clearly separable
functional components — ingestion, comparison, post-processing, filtering, and export —
each of which could be developed, tested, and refined independently before being integrated
into the full pipeline:



Department of Computer Science & Engineering ​                                               32
IU/IITE/CSE/2026/UDP-028​                          ​                    SYSTEM ANALYSIS

Why Iterative? The requirements of the reconciliation pipeline evolved during development
as edge cases such as one-to-many field mappings, composite key handling, and
reclassification logic were discovered.
An iterative model allowed these refinements to be incorporated into existing modules
without disrupting the overall system.
Process Flow The application follows a two-stage execution model that maps directly to its
process structure:
   ●​ Stage 1 – Comparison: Data is ingested, schemas are analyzed, reconciliation is
        configured, and the core field-level comparison is executed to produce statistics and
        mismatch DataFrames.
   ●​ Stage 2 – Post-Processing: The Stage 1 outputs are filtered, reclassified, transformed
        into standardized output tables, and exported to the target database along with the
        saved configuration.
Key Characteristics of the Process Model
   ●​   Each stage produces a well-defined output that serves as the input to the next stage,
        ensuring clean boundaries between processing phases.
   ●​   All configurations are persisted at the end of each run, enabling any reconciliation job
        to be reproduced or audited at a later point.
   ●​   The modular design allows individual components such as the filter engine or
        reclassification engine to be updated independently without impacting the rest of the
        pipeline.

   ●​ Session-Based Stateful Processing : Each consultation is treated as a structured
      session, where audio, transcription, and summarization data are linked and managed
      cohesively. This ensures consistency across all stages of processing and enables
      recovery or continuation in case of interruptions.

   ●​ Modular and Scalable Architecture : The system is designed with a modular
      architecture, allowing individual components such as transcription, summarization, or
      database modules to be updated independently. This ensures scalability and flexibility
      for future enhancements such as multi-language support, advanced analytics, or
      integration with large-scale hospital systems.

6.2 SYSTEM / BACKEND MODULE


The backend (port 8080) manages all persistent data operations, authentication, and
coordinates between the frontend and room agent

   ●​ Data Models : Ten SQLAlchemy models: Patient + MedicalHistory (1:1,
      demographics and history), Visit (core encounter — holds transcript ephemeral in

      super_admin (full access), admin (clinic management), room_device (scoped to

      are rejected.

   ●​ Key Visit Logic : On approval: structured summary saved to Visit.summary.
      Visit.transcript and Visit.dialogue set to null — PHI erased. Conditions and
      medications from clinicalSnapshot and doctorActions extracted and merged into
      Patient.MedicalHistory with case-insensitive deduplication.

   ●​ Room Agent (Port 8000) : The room agent runs the AI pipeline independently. A
      background job queue (in-memory, single worker thread per server) processes audio
      submissions asynchronously. Pipeline: audio received → VAD trim → WhisperX
      transcribe → word-level align → pyannote diarize → silhouette speaker count →
      transcript merge → phi4-mini summarize → fuzzy validation → result stored →
      frontend notified. Models are pre-warmed at server startup to eliminate cold-start
      latency during actual consultations.

9.1 LIMITATIONS


While the current version of the Electronic Medical Record System (Alvyto) is a fully
functional system that successfully meets its core objectives to achieve during the course of
MVP - 1, it has several limitations inherent to its design as a locally-deployed, batch-oriented
reconciliation platform.
   1.​ Processing Latency on CPU-Only Hardware : The end-to-end pipeline (audio stop
       to summary displayed) takes approximately 70–100 seconds on CPU-only clinic
       servers — comprising WhisperX transcription (~30–45s), pyannote diarization
       (~15–20s), and phi4-mini inference (~30s). This requires the doctor to remain at the
       room PC for approximately 1–2 minutes post-consultation. A GPU-equipped server
       reduces total latency to under 45 seconds, which is the recommended production
       configuration.
   2.​ Audio Quality Dependency : Transcription accuracy is sensitive to background
       noise, microphone quality, and overlapping speech. Significant ambient noise in exam
       rooms or poor microphone placement can degrade WhisperX output, which in turn
       reduces diarization accuracy and summary quality.
   3.​ Domain Vocabulary Coverage : WhisperX small.en performs well on general
       clinical English but may struggle with highly specialized medical terminology, rare
       drug names, or heavy accents. phi4-mini's zero-inference constraint means it will not
       hallucinate unfamiliar terms — but it may also fail to extract them if transcription has
       corrupted the input.
   4.​ Single-Clinic Deployment : The current architecture supports one clinic per
       deployment instance. Multi-location clinic chains require separate deployments,
       which is the correct approach for PHI isolation but requires operational management
       of multiple instances.
   5.​ Security    Completeness      : The    current   implementation     does not include
       transport-layer encryption (HTTPS), full HIPAA compliance mechanisms, or formal
       penetration testing. These are production requirements before deployment in
       regulated healthcare environments.

   6.​ Limited Concurrent Session Support : The single-worker job queue on the room
       agent processes one audio submission at a time. A clinic with many simultaneous
       room recordings would require a multi-worker queue or horizontal scaling of the
       room agent service.
9.2 FUTURE ENHANCEMENTS


The limitations of the current version provide a clear and exciting roadmap for future
enhancements that could elevate Moodify into a production-grade, commercial-quality
wellness application.
   1.​ GPU-First Deployment with Hardware Specification : Formalise an NVIDIA
       GPU (RTX 3060 minimum) as a recommended deployment requirement, reducing
       total pipeline latency to under 45 seconds and making the doctor wait time tolerable
       without any architectural changes.

   2.​ VAD-Based Pipeline Optimization : Implement Silero VAD preprocessing to strip
       silence from recordings before WhisperX processing, reducing transcription input by
       30–40% and cutting pipeline time proportionally.

   3.​ Incremental Summarization : Begin Qwen/phi4-mini summarization on the first
       60% of the transcript while diarization processes the remainder, then merge results.
       Estimated saving: 40–60 seconds on the critical path.

   4.​ Mobile Review Interface : A lightweight mobile application for doctors to review
       and approve summaries from their personal device — addressing the workflow
       challenge of doctors moving between rooms in North American clinic configurations.

   5.​ HL7 FHIR Integration : Implement export of approved summaries to standard
       healthcare interoperability formats, enabling integration with hospital-scale EMR
       systems.

   6.​ Multi-Language Support : Extend WhisperX to multilingual models (Whisper
       medium or large) to support clinical environments with non-English-speaking
       patients, particularly relevant for diverse urban clinic populations.

10.1 CONCLUSION


Alvyto demonstrates that a fully local, hallucination-controlled AI clinical documentation
system is practical on commodity clinic hardware. By integrating WhisperX transcription,
pyannote.audio diarization, and phi4-mini summarization into a single pipeline — and
enforcing a zero-inference rule with fuzzy-matching validation at every step — the system
produces structured clinical records that a doctor can review, correct, and approve in under
two minutes.


The formal four-model benchmark was a critical contribution of this project. The discovery
that Qwen3:4b's hybrid thinking architecture is fundamentally incompatible with
token-budgeted structured output tasks — consuming its entire budget on internal reasoning
traces before producing any content — is a practically significant finding for anyone
deploying small LLMs in constrained-output applications. phi4-mini's selection was justified
by measurable evidence: a perfect support rate of 1.00 across all test cases, 100% schema
compliance, and warm inference time statistically identical to the faster baseline model.


The PHI minimization design — where raw transcripts are held in-memory client-side, never
written to the database, and erased after approval — represents a principled approach to
patient data protection that goes beyond minimum requirements. The immutable audit log
and server-side JWT revocation complete a security posture appropriate for a system
handling sensitive clinical data.


The primary remaining challenge is end-to-end pipeline latency: 70–100 seconds on
CPU-only hardware requires the doctor to wait at the room PC post-consultation. A
GPU-equipped server resolves this, and is the recommended deployment configuration. The
incremental summarization and VAD optimizations identified for future work could further
reduce latency without hardware changes.




Department of Computer Science & Engineering​                                               64
IU/IITE/CSE/2026/UDP-028​                        ​                            CONCLUSION

Alvyto represents a meaningful step toward AI-powered clinical documentation that is
accurate, privacy-preserving, and deployable in the resource-constrained environments of
real-world small-to-medium clinics

Beyond its immediate clinical utility, Alvyto establishes a replicable blueprint for responsible
AI deployment in regulated domains. The architectural decisions made here —
zero-inference constraints, human-in-the-loop approval gates, PHI minimization by design
rather than by policy, and immutable audit trails — are not Alvyto-specific innovations. They
are transferable patterns that any team building AI-assisted tooling for healthcare, legal, or
financial environments can adopt. The benchmark methodology, in particular, offers a
concrete template: evaluate candidate models not on general benchmarks but on the exact
prompts, token budgets, and output schemas the production system will use.

Looking further ahead, the limitations documented in this report point toward a natural
second phase of development. GPU-first deployment with Silero VAD preprocessing and
incremental summarization could compress end-to-end latency to under 30 seconds,
effectively eliminating the post-consultation wait entirely. Integration with HL7 FHIR
standards would allow Alvyto-generated records to flow directly into hospital-scale EMR
infrastructures, extending its reach beyond independent clinics into larger healthcare
networks. Multi-language transcription support, enabled by switching to Whisper medium or
large variants, would make the system viable in linguistically diverse clinical environments
where English-only ASR currently excludes a significant patient population.

Ultimately, the most significant validation of Alvyto's design is not its benchmark scores but
its constraint: the system is engineered to fail safely. If the AI produces an unsupported
claim, it is flagged — not silently committed. If the transcript is not approved, it is erased —
not retained. If a token is reused after logout, it is rejected — not honored. These
negative-space design choices, the things the system deliberately refuses to do, are what
make it trustworthy enough to sit in a clinical environment. As AI systems take on
increasingly consequential roles in healthcare, this kind of principled restraint may prove to
be the most important engineering contribution of all.

      a clinical productivity and automation model, designed primarily for deployment

   ●​ Value Proposition : The system eliminates the need for manual note-taking and
      post-consultation documentation by automatically converting doctor–patient
      conversations into structured medical records in real time. By integrating
      transcription, summarization, and record management into a single platform, Alvyto
      reduces cognitive load on doctors, minimizes documentation errors, and ensures
      standardized clinical records. Additionally, it improves patient interaction quality by
      allowing doctors to focus entirely on consultations rather than administrative tasks.
   ●​ Target Users : The primary users of the system include:
